Understanding the energy sources that can cause a fire when near flammable materials.

üî• The Fire Triangle

A fire requires three elements to start and sustain itself. Eliminating any one side of the triangle prevents fire.

  • **Fuel:** The flammable material (e.g., wood, paper, gasoline).
  • **Oxygen:** The air or an oxidizing agent.
  • **Heat (The Ignition Source):** The energy needed to raise the fuel to its ignition temperature. [Image of the Fire Triangle diagram]

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Ignition Sources and Risks

These sources are responsible for the vast majority of accidental fires:

Source Type Examples Risk Mitigation
**Open Flames & Heat** Candles, Pilot Lights, Gas Stoves, Torches, Fireplaces Keep a minimum of 3 feet (1 meter) clearance from combustibles. Extinguish fully when unattended.
**Electrical Faults** Frayed Wires, Overloaded Sockets, Defective Appliances, Short Circuits Inspect wiring regularly. Do not run cords under rugs. Use GFCI outlets in wet areas.
**Smoking Materials** Cigarettes, Cigars, Matches, Lighters Smoke only in designated areas. Ensure all materials are cold before disposal.
**Hot Surfaces** Heaters, Light Bulbs, Motors, Exhaust Manifolds, Hot Cooking Oil Allow equipment to cool before storing. Use guards around heat-generating appliances.
**Static Electricity** Friction between materials (e.g., transferring flammable liquids) Use proper grounding and bonding procedures when handling flammable liquids and powders.

üõë Essential Prevention Rules

  1. **Maintain Distance:** Always keep ignition sources well away from any stored flammable liquids or solids.
  2. **Regular Inspection:** Have electrical systems and heating appliances inspected by qualified professionals annually.
  3. **Control the Source:** Never leave open flames or heat-generating tools unattended.
  4. **Training:** Ensure all personnel handling flammable materials or high-energy equipment are properly trained in safety protocols.
